Hi, my name is Mason D. Ferrell!

I am from middle Tennessee. I earned my Bachelor of Science in Biology with a minor in Geology from the University of Tennessee at Martin in 2022. I am currently working on my Masters thesis in Biology at Austin Peay State University under the guidence of Dr. Mollie F. Cashner.

My interest is ichthyology, specifically early life history developmental stages of North American minnows. The southeastern United States is the capital of North American minnow diversity with numerous described species. However, there are many questions regarding larval devlopment that need to be address. This is why I have chosen this area as my research focus. Also, larval development in minnows is just cool!

I am also an aquarium hobbiest with 10+ years of experience. My favorite group of fish to keep are African Cichlids from Lake Malawi. I am an enthuesiastic fish geek who will happily talk about anything related to fish for hours!

Current Research

My Masters thesis is investigating how elevated conductivity impacts larval growth and devleopment in the federally threatened Blackside Dace (Chrosomous cumberlandensis). I also plan to provide a larval description for Blackside Dace.

I also am currently working on describing morphological larval development stages in captive spawned Southern RedBelly Dace (Chrosomous erythrogaster) at Austin Peay State University. This descriptive information will be used as a model for my thesis project.

Past Research Experience

Assessed diet, age, and condiction factor of Bluegill Sunfish (Lepomis macrochirus) in reconstructed streams compared to chanelized streams in west Tennessee. This research has been presented at Association of Southeastern Biologist and Tennessee Academy of Science. Also have experience in single colony isolation with environmental yeast samples.

Field Experience

Backback electrofishing, boat electrofishing, conducting stream surveys,kick seining, measuring water quality, data collection, fish ID, fin clipping, and preserving specimens.
